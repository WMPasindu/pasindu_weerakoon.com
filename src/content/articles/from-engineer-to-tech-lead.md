# From Engineer to Tech Lead: What Actually Changes

The promotion to technical lead is deceptive. Your title changes, but the real
shift is in *where your impact comes from*. It is no longer the code you write —
it is the decisions you enable and the people you grow.

## Your output is now the team's output

As an engineer, a productive day meant merged pull requests. As a lead, a
productive day might mean **zero** commits and three unblocked teammates. That
reframing is uncomfortable at first.

> You are no longer paid to be the smartest person in the room. You are paid to
> make the room smarter.

## The skills nobody tells you to build

- **Writing.** Decisions that aren't written down don't scale. Design docs are
  how you think at the speed of a team.
- **Delegation.** If only you can do a task, that's a risk, not a flex.
- **Saying no.** Protecting focus is a core part of the role.

## Stay technical, but change altitude

You should still read code and review architecture — but you zoom out. Spend
your technical energy on the **decisions that are expensive to reverse**:

1. Data models
2. Service boundaries
3. The team's tooling and feedback loops

## Grow people deliberately

The most lasting thing you will build is not a system. It is the engineers who
become leads themselves. Give them real ownership, let them make safe mistakes,
and tell them what you see in them before they see it.

The transition is hard because the scorecard changes. Embrace it, and you trade
the satisfaction of solving one problem for the leverage of solving many.
